title: The enclosure claim
description: How the attention economy encloses attention — drawing the parallel between historical enclosure of land and the enclosure of collective attention.
---

The attention economy encloses something that was previously held in common, redirecting collective attention toward extraction rather than mutual flourishing.

## The attention economy

The concept of an "attention economy" names the structural reality that platforms and advertising technologies are built around the capture and sale of human attention. Several researchers and practitioners have developed this critique in depth.

James Williams, in *Stand Out of Our Light*, argues that the attention economy undermines the conditions necessary for human agency — not just by distracting, but by degrading the capacity for the kind of sustained, reflective attention that makes self-directed action possible. Matthew Crawford, in *The World Beyond Your Head*, traces the phenomenology of attention under assault: how designed environments — from slot machines to open-plan offices to smartphone interfaces — compete for the limited attentional resources that give a person their grip on reality. The **Center for Humane Technology** (Tristan Harris and colleagues) has brought this analysis into public policy debate, arguing that the architecture of major platforms is deliberately designed to exploit psychological vulnerabilities in ways that damage individuals and polarise societies.

A recurring observation in this work: the harms of the attention economy fall not only on individuals but on the shared conditions of public life. Polarisation, shortened time horizons, the erosion of the capacity for difficult sustained thought — these are collective phenomena, produced by individual acts of attention-capture summed across millions of people.

## Enclosure, old and new

The analogy to the English land enclosures is not merely rhetorical.

The enclosures of the 16th through 19th centuries converted common fields — land that had been held and managed collectively by communities for subsistence farming, grazing, and related uses — into privately owned property. The result was displacement and impoverishment for the commoners who had depended on those fields, and the concentration of productive resources in fewer hands. The commons were not absent before enclosure; they were present and functioning, governed by local norms and mutual obligation. Enclosure abolished the governance structure and the shared relationship to the resource alike.

The attention-economy argument runs in parallel. Fields of attention that were previously common — the shared silence of a public space, the texture of unmediated social interaction, the conditions for contemplative practice available to ordinary people through communities and teaching lineages — have been redirected toward extraction. Platforms and advertising networks do not merely compete for attention; they reshape the environment in which attention forms. As the development log for this project puts it: Facebook and Twitter pump attention out of people like oil — a dynamic that is bad even for the shareholders, since the polarisation it produces is a collective harm no single actor profits from entirely.

The structural parallel holds: the enclosure of common land produced a dispossessed class of former commoners; the enclosure of attention produces a population whose capacity for sustained agency has been degraded. In both cases, the resource that was enclosed was not the private property of those who enclosed it.

## Alienation

A Marxist-alienation reading also fits. Alienating workers from the products of their labour was considered intrinsically harmful in classical Marxist analysis — not merely economically inefficient but a damage to the person, a severing of the worker from what their labour expressed. By the same logic, alienating people from the depth of their own attention is doing something inherently harmful: severing them from what their awareness expressed, from their own capacity for presence and meaning-making.

This reading adds a dimension the attention-economy critique sometimes lacks: it locates the harm not only in what is extracted but in what is damaged in the person. The person who cannot hold attention long enough to read, reflect, or remain present is not merely less productive; they are, in some sense, less themselves.

Viktor Shiryaev's note in the development log: this is still largely a zone of personal responsibility — no one *forces* anyone to scroll. The companies' unethical behaviour is clear; so is the fact that people engage themselves. The claim is not that individuals are passive victims, but that the structural conditions make resistance individually difficult and collectively important.

## The agentic extension

A further dimension is emerging with the proliferation of AI agents.

The transformer architecture that underlies most contemporary large language models takes its name from a 2017 paper titled "Attention Is All You Need" ([arXiv:1706.03762](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762)) — a coincidence that is more than terminological. Attention, in the technical sense, is how these systems allocate processing resources across inputs. The systems that compete most aggressively for human attention are also built on machine attention.

Rohit Krishnan's concept of "Homo Agenticus" (Strange Loop Canon) identifies a new dimension: as AI agents proliferate, each one requires human attention for oversight, direction, and decision. Even if the social media attention economy were somehow regulated tomorrow, the shared resource would continue to be consumed — now by agents. There may be an "agentic commons" problem that sits alongside the attention-economy one, where the sheer number of agents demanding a share of a finite resource depletes the commons from a different direction.

This extension is speculative and early; it is named here as an open question, not a settled argument.
