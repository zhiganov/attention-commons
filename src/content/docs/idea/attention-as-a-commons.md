title: Attention as a commons
description: The central claim that attention is a commons — what the claim means, what "commons" means, and why attention fits.

Attention can be understood as a commons: a shared resource that communities govern collectively rather than leave to individual or market forces.

## The claim

The claim has two parts. The first is descriptive: attention, and the conditions that support it, are being treated as a privately ownable commodity — enclosed by platforms and advertising technologies, extracted for profit, degraded in the process. This is the "attention economy" thesis, documented by researchers and practitioners from James Williams to Matthew Crawford to the Center for Humane Technology.

The second part is constructive: a counter-tradition exists. Contemplative communities, teaching lineages, and practice groups have long governed the conditions for attention as shared resources — held through mutual accountability, transmitted through teacher-student relationships, sustained by shared norms about silence, practice, and presence. This is not a new proposal; it is a naming of something already present.

The argument is not that attention itself can be owned or divided. It is that the *conditions* for attention — silence, practice, transmitted wisdom, shared norms — can be governed commonswise, and that this form of governance has historical precedents worth studying.

## What "commons" means

A commons is a resource managed collectively by a defined community, according to shared rules, rather than by a market (private ownership) or a state (public ownership). This is the third way Elinor Ostrom demonstrated was viable, earning her the 2009 Nobel Prize in Economics. Her eight design principles for long-enduring commons describe the structural features that allow communities to manage shared resources sustainably across generations.

David Bollier extends Ostrom's framework to argue that commoning is fundamentally a *social practice* and a process of subjectivation, not only a resource-management technique. The commons is not a thing; it is what happens when a community actively tends a shared resource together. "Free, Fair and Alive" (Bollier) develops this as a political and relational claim: commoning produces a different kind of social subject, not the isolated consumer or the abstract citizen.

The P2P Foundation (Michel Bauwens and collaborators) has extended commons theory to knowledge and digital resources: Wikipedia is cited as a rare successful *global* knowledge commons, balancing centralised infrastructure with a non-profit mission focused on the shared resource itself.

## Why attention

Attention meets several of the conditions that make something a candidate for commons governance. It is a shared condition — every person's capacity for presence and agency is shaped by the cultural, relational, and material environment they inhabit. It is subject to depletion and degradation: environments saturated with addictive feeds, inflammatory content, and competing demands make sustained attention harder for everyone. And it has been held in common before, in the form of contemplative communities and teaching lineages with explicit governance structures.

The conceptual challenge is that classical commons are local and bounded, while attention is universal. The resolution developed in this project is layered:

At the **institutional level**, the commons is the contemplative community: a bounded group with shared norms, mutual accountability, and a shared resource it tends together — not land but the conditions for attention. Teacher-student lineage transmission is a form of commons governance; it maps onto Ostrom's criteria.

At a second level, the resource held in common is **the wisdom about attention** — practices, maps, discoveries accumulated across traditions, held openly like open-source software for inner development, with attribution to sources and lineages preserved.

At a deeper level, **attention as ground or birthright** is inexhaustible — never actually enclosed, only obscured. This level is not a commons in the governance sense; it can only be recognised, not managed.

The layered framing is provisional: it is one way of holding the tension between the universality of attention and the locality of successful commons governance. The fuller argument is being developed in [active-solidarity#12](https://github.com/zhiganov/active-solidarity/issues/12).

## An integral note

Ken Wilber's four-quadrant framework — individual/collective × interior/exterior — offers a useful coordinate system. The attention economy operates primarily in the collective-exterior quadrant: it is an institutional and technological structure acting on behaviour. Classical mindfulness sits in the individual-interior quadrant. The work Attention Commons is pointing toward occupies the two collective quadrants: a shared culture or language for attentiveness (collective interior) and institutional stewardship between the wellness market and isolated monasteries (collective exterior). These are, as Viktor Shiryaev's four-quadrant map identifies them, the underoccupied zones.
