---
title: The three directions
description: The three directions Attention Commons moves in — conceptual ground, practice commons, and civic and cultural movement.
---

Attention Commons pursues three complementary directions that reinforce each other: building conceptual foundations, cultivating a practice commons, and supporting a wider civic and cultural movement.

## Conceptual ground

The first direction is an argument: that attention can be understood as a commons, not only as a private cognitive capacity or a commodity to be extracted.

Classical commons theory — from Elinor Ostrom's work on governing shared resources to David Bollier's account of commoning as a social practice — shows that communities have long managed shared resources through local governance rather than leaving them to market or state. The conceptual work here is asking whether attention, and the conditions that support it, can be understood in the same frame.

This involves resolving a genuine tension. Classical commons are bounded and local — Ostrom's point is precisely that successful commons governance depends on defined membership and mutual accountability. Attention is universal. The resolution developed in this project is layered: at the institutional level, contemplative communities (a sangha, a practice group, a teaching body) *are* bounded commons — they tend not land but the conditions for attention (silence, shared practice, transmitted knowledge, relational norms), and they map onto Ostrom's design criteria. At the deeper level, attention-as-ground or birthright is inexhaustible and never actually enclosed — it can only be recognised, not managed.

The guiding distinction: commons can be governed; ground can only be recognised.

## Practice commons

The second direction is an ethos, modelled on Creative Commons but applied to contemplative and attention-training practice.

Practices that cultivate attention have been accumulated across traditions — in contemplative lineages, teaching bodies, and communities of practice — over millennia. They carry attribution: sources, lineages, teachers, transmission chains. The proposal here is that these practices should remain accessible, transmissible, and adaptable, while retaining their attribution — not enclosed by any single tradition or institution, and not dissolved into anonymous wellness content severed from its roots.

A working analogy: open-source software with attribution requirements. A working example already in existence: Ken McLeod's entire body of Vajrayana teaching at [Unfettered Mind](https://unfetteredmind.org/first-steps/), released under a Creative Commons licence (attribute, don't alter, don't profit).

The practice commons direction asks: what would it look like to produce reusable, downloadable, runnable-offline practice materials — attributing lineages, usable without gatekeepers — on the model of Climate Fresk (open-source climate workshop) rather than the model of a course platform?

## Civic & cultural movement

The third direction is an invitation: to meditation teachers, facilitators, educators, community builders, and attention practitioners to treat attention as something we can steward together.

Culture of Attention (Культура внимания) is the outward-facing name for this movement. The analogy invoked is how hygiene and mental health were made visible as public concerns — not as elite practices, not as consumer products, but as things a society can take collective responsibility for. The proposal is that a *culture of attention* already exists, stretching from the neuroscience of basic focus to the deep science of advanced contemplative states, and that naming it as a culture — making it visible as one continuous field — is itself a civic act.

This direction does not assume a finished answer. It is a call to treat the stewardship of attention as a legitimate civic and cultural question, alongside questions about public health, urban environments, and digital infrastructure.
